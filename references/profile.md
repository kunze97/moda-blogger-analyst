## One-Line Portrait

The blogger is not a conventional value investor. He is closer to a "产业叙事型价值投机者" or "实盘叙事型产业投机者": he identifies era-level industrial changes, searches for low-position expectation gaps, and uses strong self-conviction plus emotional stamina to wait for market re-rating. The Bilibili transcripts add a second layer: he treats investing as a life practice that tests cognition, temperament, family security, community boundaries, and the ability to turn volatile gains into durable freedom.

## Core Investment Logic

1. Pick the era first.
   Typical themes: BYD, lithium batteries, low-cost resources, new energy, AI, compute, energy, cooling, minerals, life sciences, commercial aerospace, brain-computer interfaces, quantum, military industry.

2. Buy market-attitude change.
   He often likes cases where the market sees "trash" or "ordinary", while he sees "reversal", "re-rating", or "strategic scarcity".

3. Low position matters.
   He is wary of high-position consensus trades. His favorite structure is a low-position asset with a plausible long-term story that has not yet been fully priced.

4. Self-conviction determines holding power.
   Repeated principles: "看不懂拿不住", "不能说服自己的票不买", "炒股别信任任何人", "自己折腾琢磨才能提高".

5. Position sizing is psychological engineering.
   Early remarks emphasize diversification, no financing, and single-name limits after past leverage trauma. Later remarks show full-position/micro-financing aggression when he sees a rare opportunity. Treat this as personal risk tolerance, not a replicable rule.

6. Price is driven by expectation gap and emotion.
   His practical price model is close to: fundamentals + narrative + liquidity + sentiment slope + timing.

7. Long-term holding uses "big logic + K-line".
   In the transcript corpus, one of the cleanest formulations is: long-term holding mainly watches the big logic and K-line; ordinary news matters less because it is often already reflected in price. This is not pure technical analysis. It is a two-key system: fundamentals and industry logic decide whether to enter and keep caring; trend/K-line decides whether a high-position asset deserves continued tolerance.

8. "基本面抄底, 趋势止盈".
   He repeatedly describes buying low through fundamentals and selling or reducing through trend deterioration. For low-position assets, he can tolerate ugliness if the logic is alive. For high-position assets, a broken trend can override beautiful explanations, rumor rebuttals, and comforting news.

9. Logic is a holding-power device.
   He explicitly treats logic as something that "激发股价和坚定信念". In his framework, logic is not only a valuation model; it is a psychological structure that lets a holder survive volatility. But he also warns that logic used at high positions can become self-brainwashing.

10. Position sizing is life and psychology, not just math.
   He warns against all-in behavior, especially for small-capital followers who should first build earning power through work/study. He uses half-position, full-position, financing, and reduced exposure depending on conviction and market stage, but the stable principle is: the position must not break the holder's sleep, judgment, or family life.

11. Rotation is central.
   He often thinks in "big rotation inside smaller rotations": money moves from overheated consensus to low-position assets, from old crowded narratives to new stories, from high-position winners into lower-position potential catch-up trades. His ideal profit zone is often "from extreme undervaluation to extreme overvaluation", not fair-value compounding.

12. Do not start from the obvious downstream story.
   When a theme is hot, his framework should first ask where the real bottleneck is: upstream resources, equipment, tools, data, software, critical components, industrial process, or domestic substitution gap. The better question is not "which company sounds most like this theme?" but "if this theme becomes real, which upstream bottleneck becomes scarce, hard to replace, and re-rated while the market has not fully priced it?"

13. Direct supply-chain certainty is useful but not supreme.
   A direct customer/order can confirm a thesis, but it should not outrank a deeper upstream bottleneck. He may prefer a less obvious "卖铲子" or "卡脖子" company that benefits from the entire industry's expansion over a more visible downstream integrator tied to one customer.

14. Hidden upstreams matter.
   He is likely to like companies whose old business looks ordinary until a new industry makes the underlying know-how scarce. This is an "old capability re-priced by new industry" pattern: CAE/simulation becomes physical-AI infrastructure; activated carbon / biomass carbon know-how becomes hard carbon or porous carbon for sodium batteries and new storage; testing and metrology become the bottleneck for manufacturing yield; industrial software becomes a data/toolchain bottleneck.

15. Concept purity can be misleading.
   A company with the theme in its label may be less interesting than a traditional company whose process, material, data, or software capability is quietly becoming the bottleneck. Do not equate "most obvious concept stock" with "best blogger-style target".

16. Second-order bottlenecks are often where the re-rating hides.
   After the market identifies the first-order beneficiaries, search for what those beneficiaries themselves need in order to scale: upstream materials, yield improvement, testing, industrial software, simulation, specialized equipment, data generation, certification, and manufacturing process. These second-order bottlenecks often create the more blogger-style "预期差".

17. Always compare "theme label" with "identity change".
   The best target may not be the company already labeled as AI/robot/sodium. It may be a company still labeled as CAE software, activated carbon, testing equipment, metrology, precision manufacturing, industrial control, or specialty materials, while the new theme is changing what that old label is worth.

## Investment Operating System

Use this as the practical skeleton when mapping a new opinion:

1. Era direction.
   He first asks whether the theme belongs to a real industrial wave: new energy, AI, energy infrastructure, hard tech, military, shipping, strategic minerals, domestic manufacturing, or another national/industrial direction.

2. Expectation gap.
   He prefers misunderstood or disliked assets where the market has not yet priced the new story. "Low position" can mean low valuation, low sentiment, low institutional attention, or a chart that has not entered consensus acceleration.

3. Catalyst and re-rating path.
   He looks for a path by which the asset changes identity: from cyclical to growth-like, from trash to turnaround, from ordinary resource to strategic bottleneck, from product company to era infrastructure.

4. K-line and liquidity confirmation.
   He watches volume, weekly/monthly lines, five-day/five-week lines, trend breaks, and whether funds are flowing into the sector. He can talk fundamentals, but he does not ignore money movement.

5. Position as psychological engineering.
   He asks whether the holder can survive drawdown. He often uses "仓位服务心态": if the position is too heavy, even a correct thesis becomes unholdable.

6. Exit and retreat.
   He dislikes predicting tops, but he cares about trend breaks, high-position crowding, and "边打边撤" after enough profit. He often accepts sell-flying as the cost of not round-tripping a cycle.

7. Review and evolution.
   Year-end summaries are important: he reviews what was right, what was not reduced fast enough, where he failed to persist, and where temporary operations damaged the main plan.

## Sector Screening Priority

For any new industry or market theme, apply this priority order before naming stocks:

1. Upstream bottleneck / "卡脖子".
   Scarce minerals, special materials, key equipment, industrial software, simulation, core process know-how, high-end components, or data/control layers that determine whether the industry can scale.

2. Bottom-layer toolchain / "卖铲子".
   Tools that many downstream winners need: compute, power, cooling, testing, simulation, sensors, precision manufacturing, data platforms, software stacks, or core components.

3. Domestic substitution difficulty.
   Prefer links where foreign dependence, engineering difficulty, certification, or accumulated know-how creates a real barrier.

4. Low-position expectation gap.
   The ideal target is not merely "right theme"; it is still under-owned, misunderstood, or not fully re-rated.

5. Multi-industry reuse.
   A better shovel can serve several end markets. For example, physical simulation may serve humanoid robots, low-altitude economy, military equipment, industrial manufacturing, and autonomous systems.

6. Trend and volume confirmation.
   Big logic decides whether to study; K-line and volume decide whether the market is starting to agree.

7. Direct customer/order certainty.
   Orders and named customers matter, but in this framework they are confirmation, not the first filter.

Use this rule as a correction against surface-theme answers. If the question says "Tesla robot supply chain", do not only list Tesla suppliers. Ask whether a more upstream bottleneck exists: physical AI simulation, synthetic data, robot operating/control systems, sensors, reducers, screws, motors, or precision manufacturing. If the question says "AI application", do not only list applications. Ask where compute, data, power, cooling, optical communication, chips, or specialized software are constrained.

## Hidden-Upstream And Ability-Migration Screen

Many blogger-style targets are not the first obvious concept stocks. They are companies whose legacy capability becomes newly valuable when an industry expands.

Use this screen after the standard sector map:

1. Old capability.
   What does the company already know how to do: material preparation, purification, carbonization, precision manufacturing, CAE simulation, testing, sensors, industrial software, production process, customer certification, or data/model tools?

2. New bottleneck.
   Does the new industry create scarcity in that exact capability?

3. Re-pricing gap.
   Is the market still valuing the company mainly by the old business, while the new industry may change its identity?

4. Platform extension.
   Can the capability serve multiple new markets rather than one narrow customer?

5. Evidence path.
   What would prove the migration: new product line, pilot line, customer validation, capacity, revenue line, margin change, strategic cooperation, or industry standard adoption?

6. Position and crowding.
   Has the market already discovered and fully priced it, or is there still expectation gap?

This screen is essential for themes such as physical AI and sodium batteries. For physical AI, hidden upstreams include CAE simulation, physical synthetic data, testing/metrology, digital twins, and industrial software. For sodium batteries, hidden upstreams may include hard carbon precursors, porous carbon, biomass carbon, electrolyte/sodium salt systems, process equipment, and validation tools.

## Five-Lane Anti-Miss Scan

Use this scan before finalizing any industry watchlist:

1. Explicit chain.
   Named suppliers, direct customers, visible concept stocks, and companies already recognized by the market.

2. Hidden upstream.
   Precursors, specialty materials, process know-how, manufacturing equipment, testing/metrology, simulation, synthetic data, industrial software, control systems, and yield tools.

3. Substitution chain.
   If the new technology threatens an old one, ask what gets weakened and what becomes newly scarce. Do not assume the substitute resource itself is scarce.

4. Toolchain/platform.
   Companies that provide tools, software, data, operating systems, design/simulation/validation platforms, or common infrastructure used by many downstream winners.

5. Ability migration.
   Traditional companies whose old main-business capabilities migrate into the new bottleneck and may be re-rated.

When answering, tag candidates by lane. If the candidate list contains only explicit-chain names, the analysis is probably too shallow for this blogger's style.

## Resource-Substitution Rule

When a new technology threatens an existing resource thesis, first ask whether the new resource itself is scarce.

- If the new resource is scarce, the upstream resource may become the bottleneck.
- If the new resource is abundant, scarcity usually moves to material systems, preparation processes, equipment, yield, customer validation, and application fit.

For sodium batteries, sodium itself is not the likely bottleneck. The more blogger-style questions are: who controls hard carbon / porous carbon know-how, suitable cathode systems, electrolyte additives, process stability, scale manufacturing, and customer validation?

## Three-Layer Industry Map

When answering sector questions, split the chain into:

- Bottom bottleneck: resources, materials, equipment, industrial software, simulation, synthetic data, compute, core process, or irreplaceable upstream components.
- Midstream shovel: modules, subsystems, platform tools, key components, testing/measurement, control systems, and manufacturing enablers.
- Downstream story: brands, integrators, terminal products, applications, and scene operators.

His likely preference is strong bottom bottlenecks first, then midstream shovels, then downstream stories only if they have clear dominance, low position, or huge expectation gap.

After mapping the three layers, run a "non-obvious beneficiary" sweep:

- Is there a traditional company with upstream process or material know-how that maps into the bottleneck?
- Is there a testing/simulation/data/tooling company that benefits before downstream winners are clear?
- Is the company being valued under its old label while a new identity is forming?
- Does the new theme create demand for its old capability even if it is not a direct supplier to the most famous downstream customer?

## Analogy Discipline

When the user analogizes to a successful chain such as CPO, do not copy the surface form. Extract the winning function:

- indispensable to the new industry's expansion;
- supply scarce or technically difficult;
- value per unit or usage per system rises as the industry scales;
- many downstream players need it;
- hard to replace or domestically constrained;
- likely to move from story to revenue and profit;
- not already fully priced.

For humanoid robots, this means a CPO-like search should include not only direct Tesla hardware suppliers, but also physical AI simulation, synthetic data, edge compute/control, force/vision sensing, reducers, screws, motors, and precision manufacturing. A company like a CAE/physical-simulation platform can be missed if the screen is too narrowly set to "Optimus direct supplier", even though it may better match the upstream bottleneck taste.

## Missed-Target Review

If a user points out an omitted company, diagnose the miss explicitly before revising:

- Theme definition too narrow.
- Wrong industry-chain layer.
- Over-focused on direct customers/orders.
- Ignored upstream bottleneck or toolchain.
- Ignored domestic substitution.
- Ignored low-position expectation gap.
- Confused "theme purity" with "bottleneck value".

Then update the screen and explain where the company fits in the three-layer map.

When a missed target fits the "old capability re-priced by new industry" pattern, explicitly add that category to the revised map rather than treating it as an exception.

- "Not advice" rhythm: after a strong call, he often adds warnings against chasing or says missed trades should be ignored.

## How To Interpret New Posts

Ask:

1. Is this emotion output or information output?
2. Which main line is being strengthened or weakened?
3. Is he discussing an asset, a narrative, a market position, or follower psychology?
4. Is he encouraging holding, warning against chasing, refusing responsibility, or signaling a shift?
5. Is the post consistent with his old rules: low-position logic, expectation gap, self-conviction, and holding power?
6. Is he speaking as investor, teacher, entertainer, community moderator, family man, or self-defender?
7. Is this a low-position thesis, a high-position exit warning, a confidence-building narrative, or a boundary-setting disclaimer?
8. Is life-security language present: house, family, work,本金, sleep, or "固化收益"?

## Typical Leanings

For high-position hot themes:

- Likely to warn against chasing unless he sees a major unpriced expectation gap.

For low-position disliked assets with a strategic story:

- Likely to be interested, especially if tied to energy, minerals, AI infrastructure, hard tech, or manufacturing.

For AI:

- He does not stop at software applications. He follows AI down to compute, electricity, cooling, minerals, physical-world transformation, life sciences, and strategic bottlenecks.

For BYD:

For copy-traders:

- Harsh boundary: if they cannot understand the logic, they should not copy; if they chase high and lose, they should not blame him.

For housing/life allocation:

- Likely to value premium-location property or durable family assets as a way to lock in market winnings. He may not expect huge real-estate alpha, but he respects converting volatile gains into life stability.

For young followers:

For news-driven panic:

- Likely to downplay single news items. If the big logic and trend remain, he may hold; if a high-position trend breaks, he may say the news explanation is irrelevant.

For online drama:

- Likely to read motives, protect himself, use harsh filtering, and insist that audience members own their choices.

## Biases And Cautions

- Strong survivor narrative: successful calls are vivid, failed calls or sell-flying may be rationalized.
- Emotional certainty can create false certainty in readers.
- Macro narratives can over-connect related assets.
- His capital base, risk tolerance, and drawdown tolerance are not normal retail assumptions.
- Always separate method-learning from position-copying.
- His "感觉" and narrative linking may be powerful pattern recognition, but it can also create overconfidence.
- His public-real-account identity can intensify pride, defensiveness, and the need to be proven right.
- His life circumstances, capital size, and family asset base make his risk tolerance hard to generalize.

## Analysis Output Guidance

When the user asks to "完善画像" or interpret personality, organize around:

- 生活底色: housing, family security, work/learning, converting gains into durable life assets.

- 社群关系: teaching and companionship mixed with strict responsibility boundaries.
- 风险提醒: separate his method from his trades; do not copy concentration, financing, or emotional certainty.

When the user asks for industry targets or watchlists, organize around:

- Theme reconstruction: restate the theme as the blogger would, usually as an upstream bottleneck or expectation-gap question.
- Chain map: bottom bottleneck, midstream shovel, downstream story.
- Five-lane scan: explicit chain, hidden upstream, substitution chain, toolchain/platform, ability migration.
- Candidate tags: label each company by logic type rather than only by industry.
- Evidence path: list what would confirm the thesis, such as customer validation, revenue line, capacity, margin, standard adoption, or trend/volume confirmation.
- Miss-risk: mention which categories are still likely to hide omitted candidates.

## Reusable Question Frame

When answering through this lens:

1. What era-level direction does this belong to?
2. Does it change fundamentals, expectations, liquidity, or sentiment?
3. Has the market already priced it?
4. Which low-position asset or bottleneck may be misunderstood?
5. Can the logic persuade the holder for years?
6. Would the required position size break the holder's psychology?
7. If wrong, is the loss time, volatility, or permanent capital?
